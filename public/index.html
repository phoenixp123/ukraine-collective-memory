Duke de Richelieu - Odessa

A monument to one of the founders of modern Odessa, Duke de Richelieu fled the French revolution to Ukraine. He ran the city as its governor-general for 11 years, later returning to France to serve as Prime Minister after Tallyrand left the position. He returned 6 years later to live out the rest of his life in Odessa as it was his adopted home. The statue commerates his service to the city and represents Ukraine's connection to a European order beyond the influence of Russia, and today the monument is heavily protected as it is a target for Russian bombardment.

People's Friendship Arch Statue

This statue was erected in 1982 in Kyiv by the Soviet administration to commerate the reunification of Ukraine with Russia. The statue has since been dismantled at the onset of hostilities as it represents the domination of Ukraine by Russia, and the destruction of the statue is the symbolic separation of friendship between the two countries. Its destruction represents the Ukrainian's desire for identity, and its continued resistance represented subjugation by an outside force.

Kharkiv Literary Museum

The original Kharkiv Literary Museum was created in 1930s, however under Soviet rule the celebration and display of Ukrainian literature was heavily repressed. After the end of the Soviet Union, the museum was reopened and today holds a rich collection of literary works, as well as presents plays, festivals, literary screenings, and meetings with popular contemporaries that celebrate Ukrainian identity. The museum helps continue the collective memory of Ukraine, as it aims to satisfy the needs for control over representation and creates a sense of meaning that helps cope with the legacy of Russian dominance.

Cathedral of St. Sophia

The Cathedral of St. Sophia in Kyiv is an important site for Ukrainian Catholics and Orthodox alike. Constructed in the 11th century, the cathedral represents the connection with the western Catholic faith. The cathedral serves as monument to national consciousness and spirit, and this distinction from Russian orthodoxy is especially significant due the separation of Ukrainian Catholics from the Russian Orthodox church. In the recent conflict, intelligence from the Ukrainian security services showed that the cathedral is a planned target of Russian airstrikes, which represent an attempt by Russia to erase part of Ukraine's connection to the West. The distinction of Ukrainian collective memory is its religious separation from Russia, and destroying the cathedral is an attempt to attack that collective memory.

ABOUT


This site seeks to explore the relationship between Ukrainian collective memory and the Ukrainian people's experiences under Russian domination. With the recent conflict fully underway, evidence has emerged of the Russian government seeking to erase the Ukrainian identity through the banning of speaking Ukrainian, the destruction of art and cultural sites, and the use of filtration camps and forced deportation. Ultimately the aim of this site is to explore and understand the use of collective memory by the Ukrainian people and the role it has played in the preservation of their identity and autonomy.
